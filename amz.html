Summer 2017 UX Design Internship

- OVERVIEW -

*this project to subject to a non-disclosure agreement*

Challenge

Redesign a seller facing application process used by Amazon's 3rd party sellers on amazon.com marketplace.

Disclaimer

As the project is under NDA, I will only be able to talk about my internship process without sharing any details related to the specifics of the designs.

Overview

I was the sole owner of the project responsible from user exploration to scoping to designs. The project took place over a span of 12 weeks while collaborating across 4 different teams in the Marketplace at Amazon.

I successfully executed the project and received a full-time return offer upon graduation in 2018.

Type

Summer Internship

Role(s)

UX Design Intern

Duration

12 weeks

- PROCESS -

- DISCOVERY -

Deep-dived into the voices of sellers on the Amazon Marketplace by looking at experiences of internal Amazon employees, external sellers, and data.

User Research

I used various channels to step into our user's shoes to truly understand their current experience, frustrations, and pain points.

  • Interviewed internal sellers and associates
  • Explored Amazon's seller forum
  • Explored various social media, primarily seller Facebook groups

Data Dive

I worked with various business analysts, project managers, and program managers to look at quantitative data in order to ground my scoping and design choices.

I also evaluated the current system by going through each workflows and identifying areas of opportunity.

- UNDERSTANDING -

Working with what I've learned through the discovery phase, I analyzed and aggregated the data into one highly detailed customer journey and empathy map.

Using the journey map, I was able to identify holes within the current experience and developed a set of 4 design principles to help guide my design process by always keeping the users and end goals in mind.

Simple affinity mapping to identify areas of opportunity

- SCOPING -

In my project proposal, I was given a very specific problem statement, list of deliverables, and project outcomes. However, after diving into the seller voice, I ended up rescoping the project's focus as the original proposal's plan will not solve the underlying user frustrations.

- DESIGN -

Once the scope and set of deliverables were set, I started my design process. Not wanting to jump to solutions right away, I started with good old pen and paper and did multiple low fidelity wireframes. Only when I fully considered the end to end workflow, ingress and end points did I move on to higher fidelity mockups.

The final deliverable was mocked up using Sketch and prototyped in Invision.

- VALIDATION -

I was able to put my designs in front of real Amazon sellers through a study of usability testing and interviews. The research study was carefully crafted to target the right audience and minimize biases, such as filtering by seller tenure and level of experience on the platform.

The research study had a total of 8 participants for a between group study to compare the current experience to those of the new design. The research study was done by the Senior UX Researcher on my team while I observed from another room.

Observing the research study conducted by my team's Senior UX Researcher

- Reflections -

A few takeaways:

  1. Leverage feedback

    2. Don't be afraid of to ask for feedback. However, feedback can become overwhelming the more you get. Treat them as a gift and make your best judgements on how to leverage the crucial ones.

  2. Invent and simplify

    3. Advocate for the users. Don't go into your design too focused on the constraints, the request made by your PMs, and try to add "band-aids" to the current experience. Take a step back and ask yourself, "is this the best experience for our users?"

  3. Clear reasons for design decisions

    4. Design decisions should always be grounded in your research and data. You should be able to justify every decision made on your designs in terms of the user experience.

  4. No one perfect solution

    5. Sometimes you can go down a rabit hole trying to find the one perfect solution and, in turn, keeping you from creating anything. Instead, don't be afraid to start over. Iterate through your ideas to create a more robust design that covers more use cases.

  5. Build trust and relationships

    6. Being a designer, you most likely will not be working alone. Learn to build trust with your PMs and team, ask your coworkers to critique your work and brainstorm with you, check in with your manager/mentor(s) consistently and ask where you are doing well and where you can improve in.
